> how does greenpeace france manage governance, so that it's 1.5 million members don't all try to show up for annual meetings, tell the board what to do in minute detail, etc

The short answer is: they don't have 1.5 million voting members.

The key distinction is between **supporters/donors** and **statutory members (adhérents)**.

For an association of Greenpeace France's size, governance is designed around a relatively small body of legal members who exercise the rights provided by the statutes, while the much larger population of donors and supporters influences the organization politically rather than through direct corporate governance.

Specifically:

* Greenpeace France has hundreds of thousands of regular financial supporters (and claims well over a million people who support it in various ways), but only a much smaller number of legal members ("adhérents") with voting rights. ([Statista][1])
* Those members elect the board (or equivalent governing body).
* The board hires and supervises the executive management.
* Campaign priorities are developed by the professional staff within the strategic framework approved by the board, not by annual votes of all supporters.

This is actually fairly typical of large NGOs. The governance model separates:

| Group             | Typical role                                                              |
| ----------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Supporters/donors | Fund the organization, participate in campaigns, petitions, local actions |
| Statutory members | Elect directors, approve accounts, amend statutes                         |
| Board             | Strategy, oversight, appoint executive leadership                         |
| Executive staff   | Run campaigns and day-to-day operations                                   |

### Why not let everyone vote?

If every donor had full corporate rights, governance would become almost impossible.

Imagine trying to conduct an AGM with 500,000 participants:

* elections become expensive and legally complex;
* a handful of coordinated activists could flood meetings;
* every operational decision could become a political fight;
* directors would spend their time responding to motions rather than governing.

Most very large NGOs therefore deliberately separate **participation** from **governance**.

### Under French association law

French law is actually very permissive.

An association under the 1901 law is largely free to define in its statutes:

* who is a member;
* what categories of membership exist;
* which categories have voting rights;
* how directors are elected;
* quorum and proxy rules. ([Service Public][2])

Many large associations therefore create categories such as:

* supporters (no vote),
* ordinary members,
* active members,
* delegates,
* honorary members.

Only one or two of those categories may vote in the AG.

### Greenpeace internationally

The international Greenpeace structure is also representative rather than direct democracy.

National organizations appoint representatives to an international council, which elects the supervisory board of the international organization rather than having millions of supporters vote directly. ([Greenpeace][3])

## 1. How Greenpeace France chooses its voting members

I was mistaken in my previous answer.

Reading the statutes, Greenpeace France takes a rather unusual approach. **Every person who is an adhérent is entitled to vote**, but they **do not all attend the governing assembly**. Instead, they elect a representative body called the *Assemblée statutaire*. ([Greenpeace][1])

The structure looks like this:

```
All paid-up members (230,000+)
        │
        │ elect
        ▼
15+ elected representatives
+
15+ appointed experts
        │
        ▼
Assemblée statutaire (~30-40 people)
        │
        │ elects
        ▼
Board (3–9 directors)
        │
        ▼
Executive director
```

A few details I found particularly interesting:

* Every paid-up member can stand for election to the Assembly. Candidates submit a statement of motivation. ([Greenpeace][1])
* Elections are held every three years, by postal and/or electronic ballot. ([Greenpeace][1])
* The Assembly is **half elected by members** and **half appointed for their expertise**. The appointed half is selected through a nomination process intended to bring in skills the membership might not naturally elect (finance, science, law, governance, etc.). ([Greenpeace][1])
* The Assembly's powers are intentionally limited. It approves accounts, elects the board, appoints auditors, debates issues, and may make proposals, but many of its policy discussions are explicitly **consultative**. Day-to-day governance rests with the board. ([Greenpeace][1])

I think that's a fairly elegant design for an organisation with hundreds of thousands of members.

## 2. For your new advocacy association

Given everything we've discussed over the past month, I would actually **not** copy Greenpeace.

Your problem is almost the opposite.

You want:

* a genuinely democratic association;
* one that ordinary volunteers feel ownership of;
* one that is resistant to capture by a determined faction;
* one that doesn't become impossible to govern if membership suddenly doubles before an election.

Those are different constraints.

### I'd lean toward a "slow democracy"

One thing that strikes me is that most takeover attempts exploit *time* rather than *numbers*.

Someone recruits 40 friends two weeks before an AG.

If they can immediately vote:

> Congratulations. They now own the association.

The simplest defence is therefore **not** to make membership exclusive, but to make political rights mature slowly.

For example:

* anyone can join immediately;
* everyone receives newsletters, can volunteer, attend meetings, propose ideas;
* voting rights begin after 6 months (or perhaps 3 months);
* eligibility for the board begins after 1 year.

This is extremely common internationally, although less common in French associations.

It rewards sustained commitment rather than mobilisation.

---

### Separate "campaigns" from "governance"

One lesson from Greenpeace is worth copying.

Members should absolutely be able to influence campaigns.

But they shouldn't be directing staff or volunteers in minute operational detail.

So I'd distinguish:

**AG decides**

* statutes
* budget
* directors
* broad strategic orientation

**Board decides**

* annual priorities
* partnerships
* hiring
* finances
* public positions between AGs

**Operational teams decide**

* press releases
* demonstrations
* negotiations
* campaign tactics

That separation prevents every operational disagreement becoming a constitutional crisis.

---

### Give members initiative rights

One thing I would add that Greenpeace doesn't really emphasize is *bottom-up agenda setting*.

For example:

* 5% of members may require a proposal to appear on the AG agenda.
* 10% may request an extraordinary AG.
* 20 members may propose a policy resolution.

Those thresholds are large enough to filter out individual hobby horses but small enough that members retain genuine influence.

---

### Supermajorities for constitutional questions

I'd also distinguish between ordinary governance and constitutional governance.

For example:

| Decision          | Threshold       |
| ----------------- | --------------- |
| Elect board       | simple majority |
| Budget            | simple majority |
| Amend statutes    | 2/3             |
| Dissolution       | 3/4             |
| Merge association | 2/3 or 3/4      |

That prevents narrow majorities from radically changing the organisation.

---

### Board size

For the scale you're envisioning—perhaps a few hundred to a couple of thousand members over the next decade—I think **9–12 directors** is a good range.

Smaller boards become fragile if two people resign.

Larger boards often become discussion clubs.

---

## One thing I'd strongly consider

Given your experience with PAV, I'd seriously consider writing into the statutes a distinction between **ends** and **means**.

The association exists to pursue its mission.

The board is elected to choose the means.

The AG should be able to remove the board if members lose confidence in it, but should not ordinarily vote on whether a particular press release should have been sent, whether to meet an elected official, or whether to support a specific amendment to a municipal plan.

That distinction is obvious in company law but often surprisingly absent from association statutes, and its absence is one reason some French associations end up trying to govern operational details through the AGM. It also helps directors understand that they are accountable for outcomes and stewardship, not for obtaining advance approval for every tactical decision.

I think you're in a very good position to write unusually robust statutes because you've already identified many of the governance failure modes that most associations only discover after 10 years.

I'd actually think of the statutes as having four distinct jobs.

1. Establish legitimacy (why the association exists)
2. Allocate power (who decides what)
3. Protect the mission (prevent capture)
4. Stay out of operations (don't constitutionalize tactics)

Many French association statutes fail because they spend 80% of their words on procedural details and 20% on constitutional principles. I'd reverse that.

## I. Identity

The usual legal boilerplate:

* Name
* Registered office
* Duration
* Ability for the board to move the siège within France (subject to ratification if legally necessary)

Nothing interesting here.

---

# II. Purpose (Objet)

I think this is the most important article.

It should satisfy several audiences simultaneously:

* judges
* tax authorities (rescrit fiscal)
* future boards
* future members

I would avoid a laundry list of activities.

Instead I'd describe:

## The public-interest objective

Something like:

> The association acts in the general interest by contributing to the preservation and improvement of the human and natural environment through the promotion of active mobility.

That deliberately echoes the language used by French tax doctrine concerning environmental protection.

Then define active mobility broadly.

For example:

> Active mobility includes walking, cycling and other modes of transport principally powered by human energy.

Then define *why*.

The association seeks to improve:

* safety
* accessibility
* health
* environmental quality
* climate
* public space
* quality of life
* social inclusion

Notice that these are all public benefits.

Not benefits for members.

That matters for fiscal doctrine.

---

# III. Means

Instead of listing every imaginable activity, I'd say the association may pursue its purpose by any lawful means, including:

* advocacy
* public policy proposals
* consultation
* litigation where appropriate
* research
* publication
* public education
* partnerships
* citizen participation
* expert advice
* campaigns

That future-proofs the statutes.

---

# IV. Explicit non-purpose

This is something I almost never see in French statutes, but I think it's extremely valuable.

Something like:

> The association is not constituted principally for recreational, sporting, commercial or educational purposes.

Then clarify:

It may organize rides, walks, conferences, training, cultural events or educational activities when these directly support its statutory mission.

This avoids future mission drift.

It says:

"We are an advocacy organisation, not a cycling club."

That is a surprisingly important distinction.

---

# V. Independence

I would make this explicit.

The association is independent of:

* political parties
* religious organizations
* commercial interests
* public authorities

while remaining free to cooperate with any of them when consistent with its purpose.

Many respected NGOs have something similar.

---

# VI. Members

I'd distinguish carefully.

For example

Members may

* participate
* volunteer
* attend meetings
* receive information
* propose ideas

Political rights mature later.

For example

Immediately:

* member

After six months:

* voting rights

After one year:

* eligible for election

This is one of the strongest anti-capture mechanisms available, and it's completely neutral. It doesn't exclude anyone; it simply rewards sustained engagement.

You could also require that dues are current at the relevant date.

---

# VII. General Assembly

This article should answer only one question:

**What belongs to the members?**

I'd make the list exhaustive.

The AG decides:

* statutes
* election/removal of directors
* annual accounts
* budget
* strategic orientations
* dissolution
* merger

That's it.

If something isn't listed, it belongs elsewhere.

---

# VIII. Board

This is where I think you can be unusually clear.

The board governs.

The board does not administer day-to-day activities.

The board is responsible for:

* implementing the mission
* safeguarding assets
* financial oversight
* annual priorities
* hiring
* legal representation
* public positions between AGs

The board chooses means.

The AG chooses ends.

That distinction should appear explicitly.

---

# IX. Delegation

An article saying:

The board may delegate operational authority to officers, employees, volunteers or committees.

Operational decisions don't require prior member approval.

That sounds obvious, but it's astonishing how many associations end up fighting over exactly this.

---

# X. Democratic safeguards

I love your idea of member initiative rights.

For example:

20 members

→ may submit a resolution.

5%

→ may require inclusion on the agenda.

10%

→ may request an extraordinary AG.

These preserve democracy while preventing every individual grievance becoming an AG motion.

---

# XI. Elections

I'd probably specify:

* board of 9–12 members
* legal minimum of 3 serving directors to maintain continuity
* two-year terms
* staggered elections (roughly half elected each year)

I especially like your idea of terms remaining valid until successors are elected, with an outer limit if needed. That avoids the common French problem where an election delay inadvertently leaves the association without a legally constituted board.

You might phrase it as:

> Directors remain in office until the election and acceptance of their successors, subject to a maximum extension of one additional year beyond the normal term.

This provides continuity without allowing indefinite mandates.

---

# XII. Vacancies

Very important.

The board should be able to co-opt replacements.

Those replacements serve only until the next ordinary election.

Otherwise resignations create constitutional crises.

---

# XIII. Supermajorities

I'd separate ordinary governance from constitutional governance.

| Decision              | Threshold       |
| --------------------- | --------------- |
| Ordinary resolutions  | Simple majority |
| Election of directors | Simple majority |
| Statute amendments    | Two-thirds      |
| Merger                | Three-quarters  |
| Dissolution           | Three-quarters  |

That's a nice constitutional structure.

---

# XIV. Conflict of interest

I would absolutely include this.

Directors must disclose conflicts.

Interested directors abstain.

Simple.

Very helpful.

---

# XV. No private benefit

Important both philosophically and fiscally.

Assets and resources exist solely to pursue the statutory purpose.

No distribution of profits.

Reasonable reimbursement of expenses permitted.

---

# XVI. Dissolution

Standard French wording.

Remaining assets transferred to another organisation pursuing comparable public-interest purposes.

---

# One constitutional principle I'd add

This may be the most valuable clause of the entire document.

An article entitled:

**Mission and interpretation**

Something like:

> These statutes shall be interpreted so as to give effect to the association's public-interest mission. Governance structures exist to enable the pursuit of that mission while ensuring democratic accountability, continuity and independence.

This becomes the "constitutional compass."

Whenever two articles seem ambiguous, everyone—from the board to the members to a judge—knows what the statutes are trying to achieve.

---

## One more idea: distinguish "mission" from "positions"

Many advocacy organisations accumulate policy positions over time, and disagreements about them can become existential.

You could define three constitutional layers:

1. **Mission** (very stable, in the statutes): promote and defend active mobility in the public interest.
2. **Strategic orientations** (approved by the General Assembly): broad multi-year priorities, such as road safety, urban accessibility, or reducing dependence on private cars.
3. **Public positions and campaigns** (set by the board or its delegates): specific advocacy on legislation, infrastructure projects, consultations, or media responses.

That makes it clear that changing a press release or supporting a particular amendment is not a constitutional question, while changing the association's fundamental purpose is. It creates a healthy balance between democratic oversight and the flexibility an advocacy organisation needs to respond quickly to events.

Je suis d'accord avec pratiquement toutes tes propositions, avec quelques nuances de rédaction pour éviter des difficultés juridiques.

### 1. Continuité de l'adhésion

Je pense que c'est une excellente idée.

Je ne parlerais pas simplement de "6 mois" mais de :

> **six mois d'ancienneté continue en qualité de membre, sans interruption d'adhésion.**

Ainsi quelqu'un qui :

* adhère 3 mois,
* quitte l'association pendant un an,
* réadhère,

repart à zéro.

C'est simple et objectif.

---

### 2. Membres = personnes physiques

Je suis également très favorable à cette règle.

Beaucoup d'associations françaises acceptent les personnes morales, mais cela crée rapidement des situations étranges :

* une autre association adhère ;
* elle désigne un représentant ;
* puis change de représentant ;
* qui vote réellement ?
* qui est responsable comme administrateur ?

Pour une association de plaidoyer, je préférerais :

> L'association est composée exclusivement de personnes physiques.

Rien n'empêche ensuite de signer des conventions de partenariat avec d'autres associations.

---

### 3. Déclaration d'engagement des administrateurs

J'aime beaucoup l'idée, mais je ne la mettrais probablement pas exactement comme tu la formules.

Les statuts ne devraient pas imposer une bureaucratie lourde.

En revanche, je mettrais un principe.

Par exemple :

> Tous les six mois, chaque administrateur adresse au conseil une brève déclaration indiquant :
>
> * les actions qu'il a conduites au service de l'objet de l'association ;
> * les responsabilités qu'il assume actuellement ;
> * les objectifs qu'il entend poursuivre au cours des six mois suivants.

J'aime beaucoup cette idée parce qu'elle rappelle que le conseil est un organe de travail, pas un titre honorifique.

---

### 4. Absences

Je suis très favorable à une règle automatique.

Par exemple :

> Est réputé démissionnaire tout administrateur qui, sans motif légitime communiqué au conseil, est absent à trois réunions consécutives du conseil d'administration.

Cela évite énormément de conflits.

---

### 5. Révocation disciplinaire

Là je serais un peu plus prudent.

En droit français, l'Assemblée générale doit toujours pouvoir révoquer un administrateur.

En revanche, je ne donnerais pas au conseil un pouvoir discrétionnaire de destitution.

Cela devient vite politique.

Je prévoirais plutôt :

Le conseil peut constater :

* la démission,
* le décès,
* la perte de qualité de membre,
* les absences automatiques,
* les incompatibilités prévues par les statuts.

Pour le reste :

Le conseil peut suspendre provisoirement un administrateur dans des cas graves (par exemple manquement aux obligations fiduciaires, conflit d'intérêts non déclaré, comportement portant gravement atteinte aux intérêts de l'association), mais seule l'Assemblée générale prononce la révocation définitive.

C'est beaucoup plus solide juridiquement.

## Une autre idée qui me paraît très importante

Je pense que les administrateurs devraient avoir une **obligation fiduciaire** explicite.

Par exemple :

> Les administrateurs exercent leur mandat dans l'intérêt exclusif de l'association et de son objet statutaire. Ils ne représentent ni les intérêts particuliers d'un membre, ni ceux d'une organisation extérieure, ni ceux d'un parti politique, d'une entreprise ou d'une collectivité publique.

C'est très puissant.

Cela évite le syndrome :

> "Je siège au CA pour représenter mon association."

Non.

Tu sièges parce que tu es administrateur de **Chemins Communs**.

---

## Encore une idée

Je mettrais un article intitulé **Loyauté du débat**.

Quelque chose comme :

> Les décisions régulièrement adoptées par les organes compétents engagent l'association. Les administrateurs demeurent libres d'exprimer leurs opinions lors des délibérations internes, mais s'abstiennent de toute action de nature à empêcher l'exécution des décisions régulièrement prises, sauf si celles-ci sont manifestement contraires à la loi ou aux présents statuts.

C'est une façon élégante d'éviter les guerres internes permanentes.

---

## Enfin, je ferais une distinction entre les statuts et un règlement intérieur

Je pense que les statuts devraient rester relativement stables (une quinzaine à une vingtaine d'articles), tandis que le règlement intérieur préciserait les modalités pratiques : fréquence des réunions, format de la déclaration semestrielle des administrateurs, fonctionnement des commissions, procédures de consultation des membres, etc.

En revanche, les éléments suivants me semblent suffisamment structurants pour figurer directement dans les statuts :

* l'objet d'intérêt général et son interprétation ;
* l'indépendance de l'association ;
* l'exclusivité des **personnes physiques** comme membres ;
* les délais d'ancienneté **continue** (6 mois pour le droit de vote, 12 mois pour l'éligibilité) ;
* la répartition des compétences entre Assemblée générale, Conseil d'administration et équipe opérationnelle ;
* les initiatives des membres (inscription de résolutions, convocation d'une AG extraordinaire) ;
* les majorités qualifiées pour les décisions constitutionnelles ;
* l'obligation fiduciaire des administrateurs ;
* la déclaration semestrielle d'engagement des administrateurs ;
* la démission réputée acquise après trois absences consécutives non justifiées ;
* le maintien des mandats jusqu'à l'élection des successeurs (avec une durée maximale de prolongation) ;
* la possibilité de coopter provisoirement un administrateur jusqu'à la prochaine élection.

Je pense que cette architecture donnerait des statuts exceptionnellement solides pour une association française de plaidoyer : suffisamment détaillés pour prévenir les principaux conflits de gouvernance, mais sans enfermer l'association dans des règles opérationnelles qui devraient relever du règlement intérieur.
